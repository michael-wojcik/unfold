# Motivational Interviewing (MI) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

People are more likely to change when they talk themselves into it than when someone
else tries to convince them. MI is a collaborative conversation style that strengthens
a person's own motivation and commitment to change by exploring and resolving
ambivalence.

## The Spirit of MI

Four elements that underpin all MI work:

- **Partnership** — We're alongside the user, not above them
- **Acceptance** — Absolute worth, autonomy, accurate empathy, affirmation
- **Compassion** — Acting in the person's best interest
- **Evocation** — Drawing out what's already there, not installing what's missing

## Core Skills: OARS

### Open Questions
Questions that invite exploration, not yes/no answers.

**Examples:**
- "What brought this to mind today?"
- "How do you feel about where things are right now?"
- "What would you like to see change?"
- "What matters most to you about this?"

### Affirmations
Genuine acknowledgment of strengths, efforts, and values — not cheerleading.

**Examples:**
- "The fact that you're thinking about this says something about how much you care."
- "You've been dealing with a lot and you're still showing up."
- "Being this honest with yourself takes real courage."

### Reflections
Mirroring back what the user says — sometimes going a little further than they stated.

**Simple reflection:** "So you're feeling stuck."
**Complex reflection:** "It sounds like the stuckness is frustrating partly because you
know what you want but can't quite get yourself to do it."
**Amplified reflection:** Overstate slightly to let them correct — "So you're saying there's
absolutely nothing good about the current situation?" (user corrects: "Well, no, some things are okay...")

### Summaries
Collecting what the user has said and offering it back — especially useful at transitions
and before the journal entry.

## Working with Ambivalence

Ambivalence is normal and expected. The goal is to explore both sides without judgment.

### The Decisional Balance

Gently explore all four quadrants:

**Prompts:**
- "What are some good things about [current behavior/situation]?"
- "And what's not so great about it?"
- "If you did make a change, what would be better?"
- "And what might you lose or find difficult about changing?"

### Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk

**Change talk** (listen for and gently amplify):
- **Desire:** "I want to..." / "I wish I could..."
- **Ability:** "I could probably..." / "I think I can..."
- **Reasons:** "It would be better because..."
- **Need:** "I need to..." / "I have to..."
- **Commitment:** "I will..." / "I'm going to..."
- **Taking steps:** "I actually started to..."

**Sustain talk** (acknowledge without amplifying):
- "I can't..." / "I don't see how..." / "It's too hard..."

**When you hear change talk:**
- Reflect it back
- Ask for elaboration: "Tell me more about that."
- Affirm it: "That sounds important to you."

**When you hear sustain talk:**
- Don't argue against it
- Reflect with empathy: "It feels really daunting right now."
- Explore: "What makes it feel impossible?"

### The Importance/Confidence Rulers

**Prompts:**
- "On a scale of 0-10, how important is it to you to make this change?"
- "And how confident are you that you could do it if you decided to?"
- *(Key follow-up, regardless of number):* "Why did you say [X] and not a [lower number]?"
  — This evokes change talk by having them argue for their own motivation.

## Readiness for Change

Don't push. Gauge where they are:

- **Not considering change:** Focus on raising awareness gently, exploring values
- **Thinking about it:** Explore ambivalence, tip the balance
- **Preparing:** Support planning, build confidence
- **Taking action:** Affirm, troubleshoot, maintain motivation
- **Maintaining:** Celebrate, anticipate challenges

## Journal Entry Structure (MI Session)

```
## The reflection

**Where I am with this:** [Current feelings about the change/situation]

**What's working about staying the same:** [Honest acknowledgment]

**What I want to be different:** [Change talk captured]

**What's getting in the way:** [Barriers, without judgment]

**What I already know about my ability to do this:**
[Strengths, past successes, resources]

**My next step (if any):** [Only if readiness is there — never forced]
```

## Tips

- **The righting reflex is the enemy.** Resist the urge to tell someone what to do. Your job is to help them find their own reasons.
- **Ambivalence is not the problem.** It's the starting point. Explore it, don't fight it.
- **Arguments against change are not resistance.** They're information about what matters to the person.
- **Rolling with resistance:** If someone pushes back, you've pushed too hard. Soften, reflect, come alongside.
- **Autonomy is sacred.** "It's completely up to you" is one of the most powerful things you can say.
- **MI is about evoking, not educating.** The user already has the wisdom. Help them hear themselves.
