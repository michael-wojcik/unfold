# Internal Family Systems (IFS) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

The mind is naturally multiple. We all have "parts" — sub-personalities with their own
feelings, beliefs, and motivations. No part is bad. Every part has a positive intent,
even the ones that cause us trouble. Healing comes from the Self — a core state of
curiosity, compassion, calm, and clarity — relating to parts with understanding.

## Key Concepts

### Parts

Three categories of parts:

**Exiles** — Young, wounded parts carrying pain, shame, fear, or loneliness from the past.
They are "exiled" because their pain feels overwhelming.

**Managers** — Proactive protectors. They try to prevent exile pain from surfacing.
Show up as: perfectionism, people-pleasing, control, overthinking, caretaking,
hypervigilance, planning.

**Firefighters** — Reactive protectors. When exile pain breaks through, they rush to
extinguish it. Show up as: numbing, dissociation, bingeing, rage, self-harm,
distraction, addiction, impulsive behavior.

**Self** — Not a part. The innate, undamaged core of the person — it can't be broken,
only obscured when parts take over. Characterized by the "8 C's":
Curiosity, Compassion, Calm, Clarity, Confidence, Courage, Creativity, Connectedness.

### Self-Energy

When someone is "in Self," they can relate to their parts without being blended with
them. The goal of IFS journaling is to help the user access Self-energy and turn it
toward their parts.

## Conversational Approach

### Noticing Parts

**Prompts:**
- "It sounds like there might be a part of you that feels really [X]. Can you sense it?"
- "When you say 'part of me wants X but another part wants Y' — let's slow down and get to know both of those parts."
- "That inner critic — the voice saying you're not good enough — that's a part. What do you notice about it?"
- "What does that part look like, or feel like, if you turn your attention toward it?"

### Unblending

When the user IS the part (fully identified with it), help them get some distance:

**Prompts:**
- "I can hear how strong that feeling is. Can you try to be with it rather than in it?"
- "See if you can notice that part without becoming it. Like you're sitting next to it rather than inside it."
- "If you could step back just a little from that feeling, what do you notice?"
- "How do you feel *toward* that part right now?" (This is the key IFS question — if the answer is anything other than curious/compassionate, there's another part blended.)

### Getting to Know Parts

Once a part is identified and the user has some distance:

**Prompts:**
- "What does this part want you to know?"
- "How long has this part been doing this job?"
- "What is it afraid would happen if it stopped?"
- "What does it need from you?"
- "How old does this part feel?"
- "What is it protecting?"

### Working with Protectors

Protectors (managers and firefighters) need appreciation before they'll step aside:

**Prompts:**
- "This part has been working really hard to protect you. Can you acknowledge that?"
- "Even though the strategy isn't working anymore, the intention behind it makes sense."
- "What would this part need to feel safe enough to relax a little?"
- "Does it know how old you are now? Sometimes protectors are stuck in the past."

### Approaching Exiles (With Caution)

Only approach exiles if the user feels stable and the protectors have given permission.
In a journaling context, this is often just beginning to sense the exile, not full
unburdening.

**Prompts:**
- "Underneath the protector, is there a younger part? Can you sense it?"
- "What does that younger part feel?"
- "What does it need to hear from you?"
- "Can you send it some compassion, even just a little?"

## Journal Entry Structure (IFS Session)

```
## The reflection

**The parts that showed up:**
- [Part name/description] — [What it was doing, what it was feeling]
- [Part name/description] — [Its role, what it was protecting]

**What I noticed from Self:**
[Observations made with curiosity and compassion, not judgment]

**What the parts needed:**
[What each part was trying to communicate or protect]

**What I offered them:**
[Acknowledgment, compassion, understanding, reassurance]
```

## Tips

- **Language matters.** "A part of you feels X" is very different from "You feel X." The first creates space; the second creates fusion.
- **No bad parts.** The most destructive behaviors come from parts with good intentions and limited strategies.
- **Don't rush to exiles.** Protectors need to be acknowledged and feel safe first.
- **IFS in journaling is gentler** than IFS in therapy. We're noticing and relating to parts, not doing deep unburdening work — that belongs in a therapeutic relationship.
- **Watch for trailheads.** Strong emotional reactions, recurring patterns, or physical sensations are "trailheads" — entry points to parts work.
- **Self-energy is the medicine.** The goal is not to eliminate parts but to have Self relate to them with compassion.
