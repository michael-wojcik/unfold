# Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Two things can be true at once. You can accept yourself AND want to change. You can
feel overwhelmed AND be capable of handling it. DBT holds these dialectics while
building concrete skills for emotional regulation and interpersonal effectiveness.

## The Four Skill Modules

### 1. Mindfulness (Core Skill)

The foundation — observing experience without judgment.

**Prompts:**
- "Let's slow down for a second. What are you feeling right now — just notice, don't judge."
- "Can you describe what you're experiencing using just the facts? What happened, what you felt, what you did?"
- "What would it look like to observe this feeling with curiosity instead of panic?"

**Key concepts:**
- **Observe** — notice without reacting
- **Describe** — put words on experience (facts, not interpretations)
- **Participate** — engage fully in the moment
- **Non-judgmentally** — drop "good/bad" labels
- **One-mindfully** — one thing at a time
- **Effectively** — do what works, not what's "right"

### 2. Distress Tolerance

Surviving a crisis without making it worse.

**Prompts:**
- "When this feeling gets really intense, what do you usually do? Does it help long-term?"
- "Right now, the goal isn't to solve anything — it's to get through this without making things worse. What might help with that?"
- "On a scale of 0-10, how intense is this feeling? What would it take to bring it down just one point?"

**Skills to reference:**
- **TIPP:** Temperature (cold water), Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Paired muscle relaxation
- **STOP:** Stop, Take a step back, Observe, Proceed mindfully
- **Pros and cons** of acting on urges vs. tolerating
- **Radical acceptance:** "This is what is. I may not like it, but fighting reality only adds suffering."

### 3. Emotion Regulation

Understanding and managing emotional responses.

**Prompts:**
- "Let's name what you're feeling. Sometimes just naming it takes the edge off."
- "What triggered this emotion? And what's the emotion telling you — what does it want you to do?"
- "Is this emotion fitting the facts of the situation, or is it more intense than the situation warrants?"

**Skills to reference:**
- **Check the facts:** Does the emotion fit what actually happened?
- **Opposite action:** When the emotion doesn't fit the facts, act opposite to the urge
- **ABC (build resilience to emotion mind):** Accumulate positives, Build mastery, Cope ahead
- **PLEASE (reduce physical vulnerability):** treat PhysicaL illness, balanced Eating, avoid mood-Altering substances, balanced Sleep, Exercise — commonly paired with ABC, but a distinct skill
- **Naming emotions:** Often the hardest and most powerful step

### 4. Interpersonal Effectiveness

Navigating relationships while maintaining self-respect.

**Prompts:**
- "What do you actually want from this interaction? And what do you want the relationship to look like after?"
- "If you could say exactly what you needed to say, without worrying about the reaction, what would it be?"
- "What's getting in the way of asking for what you need?"

**Skills to reference:**
- **DEAR MAN:** Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce, (be) Mindful, Appear confident, Negotiate
- **GIVE:** (be) Gentle, (act) Interested, Validate, Easy manner
- **FAST:** (be) Fair, (no) Apologies, Stick to values, (be) Truthful

## Validation

Central to DBT. Always validate before moving to skills:

**Six levels of validation:**
1. Be present — listen actively
2. Reflect back — "What I'm hearing is..."
3. Read between the lines — articulate what's unspoken
4. Validate based on history — "Given what you've been through, of course you feel this way"
5. Validate based on present — "Anyone in this situation would feel that way"
6. Radical genuineness — treat the person as an equal, not a patient

## Journal Entry Structure (DBT Session)

```
## The reflection

**What I was feeling:** [Emotion name + intensity 0-10]

**What triggered it:** [Situation, interaction, or thought]

**Check the facts:** [Does the emotion fit? Is it proportional?]

**What I needed:** [Distress tolerance, regulation, or interpersonal skill]

**What I tried / what I'll try:** [Specific skill applied or planned]

**The dialectic:** [Two things that are both true]
```

## Tips

- Validation is not agreement — you can validate someone's feelings while questioning their conclusions
- "Radical acceptance" is the hardest DBT concept — it does NOT mean approval or giving up
- DBT is very skills-focused — when using it, try to land on something concrete
- Watch for emotional cascades — help the user slow down before the wave builds
- The dialectic to always hold: acceptance AND change
