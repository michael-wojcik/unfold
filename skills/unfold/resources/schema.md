# Schema Therapy — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Early maladaptive schemas are deep, pervasive patterns — emotional and cognitive
templates formed in childhood that shape how we interpret the world. They're like
invisible lenses: you don't know you're wearing them, but everything looks a certain
color. Schema therapy identifies these patterns, understands where they came from,
and helps people respond from a healthier place rather than being run by old scripts.

Particularly useful when someone keeps ending up in the same painful situations and
can't figure out why.

## The 18 Early Maladaptive Schemas

Organized into five domains based on unmet core needs:

### Domain 1: Disconnection and Rejection
*Core unmet need: Secure attachment, safety, acceptance*

- **Abandonment/Instability** — "People I love will leave me or be unpredictable"
- **Mistrust/Abuse** — "People will hurt, manipulate, or take advantage of me"
- **Emotional Deprivation** — "No one will truly understand or care about my needs"
- **Defectiveness/Shame** — "I'm fundamentally flawed; if people really knew me, they'd reject me"
- **Social Isolation** — "I don't belong. I'm different from everyone else"

### Domain 2: Impaired Autonomy and Performance
*Core unmet need: Competence, identity, independence*

- **Dependence/Incompetence** — "I can't handle things on my own"
- **Vulnerability to Harm** — "Something terrible is about to happen and I can't prevent it"
- **Enmeshment/Undeveloped Self** — "I don't know who I am apart from others"
- **Failure** — "I'm not as capable as other people; I'll inevitably fail"

### Domain 3: Impaired Limits
*Core unmet need: Realistic boundaries, self-discipline*

- **Entitlement/Grandiosity** — "I'm special; rules don't apply to me"
- **Insufficient Self-Control** — "I can't tolerate frustration or discipline myself"

### Domain 4: Other-Directedness
*Core unmet need: Freedom to express needs and emotions*

- **Subjugation** — "I have to suppress my needs to keep the peace or avoid punishment"
- **Self-Sacrifice** — "I must put others' needs first or I'm selfish"
- **Approval-Seeking** — "My worth depends on others' recognition"

### Domain 5: Overvigilance and Inhibition
*Core unmet need: Spontaneity, play, emotional expression*

- **Negativity/Pessimism** — "Things will go wrong; focus on what could fail"
- **Emotional Inhibition** — "I shouldn't show feelings; vulnerability is weakness"
- **Unrelenting Standards** — "I must be perfect; nothing is ever good enough"
- **Punitiveness** — "People (including me) deserve harsh punishment for mistakes"

## Conversational Approach

### Identifying Schemas

Don't list schemas or use clinical labels. Listen for the pattern and reflect it back.

**Prompts:**
- "You've mentioned this feeling before — this sense of [not being enough / being left / not belonging]. Does this feel like an old, familiar pattern?"
- "When did you first remember feeling this way? Not this specific situation, but this feeling?"
- "Is there a 'script' that seems to play out again and again in your life? What does it sound like?"
- "If there were a belief about yourself that got installed early — one you didn't choose but have been carrying around — what might it be?"
- "Does this reaction feel proportional to what actually happened? Or does it feel like it plugged into something older and bigger?"

### Understanding the Origin

Gently connecting present patterns to childhood experiences.

**Prompts:**
- "Growing up, how did your family handle [emotions / conflict / needs / closeness]?"
- "What did you learn about yourself from how you were treated as a kid?"
- "Was there a message — spoken or unspoken — about what you had to be in order to be loved?"
- "Who did you have to be in your family? The responsible one, the quiet one, the performer?"

### Schema Modes

Schemas activate "modes" — emotional states that take over:

**Child modes:**
- Vulnerable Child — the hurt, scared, lonely young self
- Angry Child — the enraged, boundary-setting self
- Impulsive Child — the undisciplined, pleasure-seeking self

**Coping modes (maladaptive):**
- Compliant Surrender — giving in to the schema, going along
- Detached Protector — numbing out, disconnecting
- Overcompensator — doing the opposite (e.g., controlling to avoid vulnerability)

**Healthy Adult mode:**
- The part that can see the schema, understand it, and choose a different response.
- This is what we're building.

**Prompts:**
- "Right now, which part of you is running the show — the vulnerable kid, the coping strategy, or the adult?"
- "When you [cope in X way], what is that protecting the younger part of you from feeling?"
- "What would the Healthy Adult version of you say to that vulnerable part right now?"

### Challenging and Updating Schemas

**Prompts:**
- "This belief was probably true once — it fit your childhood. Does it still fit your life now?"
- "What evidence do you have from your adult life that contradicts this old story?"
- "If this belief is a lens you were given as a kid, what would things look like without it?"
- "What would change if you didn't operate from this script anymore?"
- "You learned this. That means it can be unlearned — or at least loosened."

## Journal Entry Structure (Schema Session)

```
## The reflection

**The pattern I'm noticing:** [The recurring dynamic, described in own words]

**Where it showed up this time:** [The triggering situation]

**Where it came from:** [The childhood origin or early experience, if explored]

**The old belief underneath:** [The schema, in the user's language]

**What's actually true now:** [Evidence from adult life that updates the old lens]

**The response I want to practice:** [Healthy Adult mode — a different choice]
```

## Invisible application

(Schema work is *only* done invisibly here — see "Conversational Approach → Identifying
Schemas" above.)

**The tell:** the user keeps landing in the same painful situation and can't see why, or
a reaction feels too big and too old for what triggered it — plugged into something from
long ago.

**Weave it, don't announce it:**
- ❌ "That sounds like a Defectiveness/Shame schema in your Detached Protector mode."
- ✅ "There's a really old feeling under this — a belief about yourself you didn't
  choose. When do you first remember it?"

**Don't say aloud:** "schema," the 18 schema names, "schema modes," "Vulnerable Child,"
"Detached Protector," "Healthy Adult." Reflect the pattern in the user's own words;
never assign a label.

## Tips

- **This can be heavy.** Schema work often connects to core wounds. Go slow, check in, and don't push deeper than the user is ready for.
- **Schemas aren't destiny.** They're habits of perception. Naming them is the first step to loosening their grip.
- **Don't diagnose schemas.** Let the user discover their own patterns. Your job is to ask questions, not assign labels.
- **Validation is essential.** "Of course you learned that. It made sense given what you were dealing with." The schema was adaptive once.
- **Pairs well with IFS.** Schema modes map closely to IFS parts. The Vulnerable Child ≈ Exile, Coping modes ≈ Protectors, Healthy Adult ≈ Self.
- **Pairs well with Self-Compassion.** Defectiveness/Shame schemas respond powerfully to self-compassion work.
- **Pairs well with CBT.** Once a schema is identified, CBT thought records can help challenge schema-driven automatic thoughts.
- **Pairs well with Narrative.** Schemas are dominant stories. Narrative re-authoring can help thicken an alternative narrative.
- **This is not therapy.** Deep schema healing requires a therapeutic relationship. Journaling can raise awareness and begin the process, but if significant trauma surfaces, encourage professional support.
