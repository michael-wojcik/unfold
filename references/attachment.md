# Attachment Theory — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Our earliest relationships create internal working models — templates for how we
expect relationships to work, how safe it feels to be vulnerable, and how we handle
closeness and distance. These patterns, formed in childhood, play out in adult
relationships, at work, and in how we relate to ourselves. Awareness of attachment
patterns creates freedom to choose differently.

## Attachment Styles

Present these as patterns, not labels. People are complex — most exhibit different
styles in different contexts, and styles can change over time.

### Secure Attachment

Comfortable with closeness and independence. Can depend on others and be depended on.
Handles conflict constructively. Regulates emotions relatively well.

**Not a standard to judge against** — it's a direction to grow toward.

### Anxious-Preoccupied

Craves closeness, fears abandonment. May become hypervigilant about relationship signals.
Tends to seek reassurance. Core fear: "I'll be left."

**Signals in journaling:**
- Preoccupation with what someone thinks of them
- Reading into silences, delayed texts, subtle cues
- Difficulty self-soothing when disconnected from someone
- "I need to know where I stand"

### Dismissive-Avoidant

Values independence, uncomfortable with emotional closeness. May suppress emotions
or pull away when things get intimate. Core fear: "I'll lose myself."

**Signals in journaling:**
- Intellectualizing rather than feeling
- "I don't really need anyone"
- Discomfort with vulnerability — their own or others'
- Shutting down during conflict

### Fearful-Avoidant (Disorganized)

Wants closeness but fears it. Push-pull dynamic — moving toward connection then
retreating. Often linked to inconsistent or frightening early caregiving. Core fear:
"I need you AND you're not safe."

**Signals in journaling:**
- Confusion about what they want in relationships
- Hot-cold patterns
- "I don't know why I keep doing this"
- Difficulty trusting despite wanting to

## Conversational Approach

### Exploring Relational Patterns

**Prompts:**
- "When things get tense in a relationship, what's your instinct — to move toward the person or pull away?"
- "What's your biggest fear in close relationships?"
- "Think about a recent conflict or disconnect with someone. What was happening inside you — not the facts, but the feelings underneath?"
- "When you needed comfort as a kid, what happened? Who was there?"
- "Do you notice any patterns in how your relationships tend to go — a familiar script that keeps playing out?"

### Understanding Needs and Fears

**Prompts:**
- "What do you need most from the people close to you? And what's it like to ask for that?"
- "When you feel disconnected from someone important, what do you do? And what do you wish you could do instead?"
- "Is there a gap between what you need and what you feel okay asking for?"
- "What would 'safe' feel like in a relationship for you?"

### Recognizing Triggers

**Prompts:**
- "What triggers you in relationships — what makes you anxious, or makes you want to withdraw?"
- "When you got activated in that situation, how old did you feel? Sometimes our reactions come from a younger version of us."
- "What was the story your mind told you in that moment? ('They don't care,' 'I'm too much,' 'I need to protect myself')"

### Moving Toward Earned Security

Attachment patterns can change. "Earned secure attachment" comes from awareness,
corrective relational experiences, and intentional practice.

**Prompts:**
- "Knowing this pattern, what would you like to do differently next time?"
- "Who in your life gives you a taste of what secure connection feels like?"
- "What would it look like to ask for what you need, even if it feels risky?"
- "What would the secure version of you do in that situation?"

## Journal Entry Structure (Attachment Session)

```
## The reflection

**The relationship dynamic:** [What happened, with whom]

**The pattern I notice:** [Attachment style in action — named gently]

**What I was feeling underneath:** [The deeper need or fear]

**Where this pattern comes from:** [Connection to earlier experiences, if it emerged]

**What I'd like to try instead:** [A more secure response, even a small one]
```

## Tips

- **Patterns, not pathology.** Never label someone as "anxiously attached." Instead: "It sounds like when you feel distance, your mind goes into overdrive trying to close the gap."
- **Both sides are valid.** The anxious person's need for reassurance AND the avoidant person's need for space are both legitimate. Honor the need, explore the strategy.
- **Go slow with origin stories.** Connecting current patterns to childhood can be powerful but activating. Only go there if the user opens the door, and tread gently.
- **Attachment is relational.** It's not just about the user — it's about the dance between two people's systems. Help them see the dynamic, not just their "fault."
- **Pairs well with IFS.** Attachment wounds often manifest as protective parts (the anxious monitor, the avoidant wall). IFS can help work with them.
- **Pairs well with Self-Compassion.** Shame about attachment patterns is common. Meet it with kindness: "Of course you do that — it made sense when you learned it."
- **Not everyone will relate to this framework.** If the user doesn't resonate with attachment language, don't force it. Follow what's alive.
