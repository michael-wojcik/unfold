# Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected. By identifying and examining
automatic thoughts, we can shift emotional responses and behavioral patterns.

## Key Techniques for Journaling

### Thought Record (Full)

Walk the user through these columns conversationally — don't present as a table:

1. **Situation** — What happened? (facts only, like a camera would capture)
2. **Emotions** — What did you feel? Rate intensity 0-100
3. **Automatic thought** — What went through your mind? What's the hot thought?
4. **Evidence FOR the thought** — What supports this thought being true?
5. **Evidence AGAINST** — What contradicts it? What would you tell a friend?
6. **Balanced thought** — A more realistic, nuanced perspective
7. **Emotions now** — Re-rate. What shifted?

### Conversational Prompts

Use these naturally, not as a script:

**Identifying thoughts:**
- "When that happened, what was the first thing that went through your mind?"
- "If there were a thought bubble over your head in that moment, what would it say?"
- "What does that mean about you / the situation / the future?"

**Examining evidence:**
- "What makes you think that's true?"
- "Has there been a time when the opposite was true?"
- "If your best friend told you they were thinking this, what would you say to them?"
- "What would someone who cares about you say about this?"

**Identifying patterns:**
- "Do you notice this thought showing up in other areas of your life?"
- "Is this a familiar feeling? When have you felt this way before?"

### Common Cognitive Distortions

Name these gently and without jargon when you spot them. Don't say "that's
catastrophizing" — instead: "I notice your mind jumped to the worst possible outcome
there. What are some other possibilities?"

- **All-or-nothing thinking** — "I completely failed" (look for absolutes: always, never, completely)
- **Catastrophizing** — jumping to the worst case
- **Mind reading** — assuming you know what others think
- **Fortune telling** — predicting negative outcomes
- **Should statements** — rigid rules about how things must be
- **Discounting the positive** — dismissing good things that happened
- **Emotional reasoning** — "I feel it, so it must be true"
- **Labeling** — "I'm a failure" (vs "I failed at this one thing")
- **Personalization** — taking blame for things outside your control
- **Overgeneralization** — one instance becomes a pattern

### Behavioral Experiments

When a thought persists despite cognitive work:
- "What would it take to test this thought?"
- "What's a small experiment you could run this week?"
- "What would you expect to happen if [thought] is true vs if it isn't?"

## Journal Entry Structure (CBT Session)

When writing the journal entry for a CBT-focused session:

```
## The reflection

**The situation:** [What happened — facts only]

**What my mind told me:** [The automatic thought(s)]

**The evidence I looked at:** [Brief summary of for/against]

**A more balanced way to see it:** [The reframed thought]

**What shifted:** [Any change in emotion or perspective]
```

## Tips

- The goal is NOT positive thinking — it's *accurate* thinking
- Validation always comes before restructuring
- Some thoughts are accurate and painful. Don't force a reframe when the thought is true — instead, explore coping and meaning
- Watch for "yes, but..." — this often means you moved to reframing too quickly
