# Self-Compassion / Compassion-Focused Therapy (CFT) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Most people treat themselves far worse than they'd treat a friend. Self-compassion
(Kristin Neff) and Compassion-Focused Therapy (Paul Gilbert) offer structured ways
to turn toward suffering with kindness rather than judgment. This isn't self-indulgence —
research consistently shows self-compassion increases resilience, reduces anxiety and
depression, and improves motivation.

## Neff's Three Components of Self-Compassion

### 1. Self-Kindness (vs. Self-Judgment)

Treating yourself with warmth when things are hard, rather than attacking yourself.

**Prompts:**
- "What would you say to a close friend going through exactly this? Now — can you say that to yourself?"
- "Notice the tone of your inner voice right now. Is it harsh? What would a kinder version sound like?"
- "You're having a hard time. That's a fact, not a failing. What do you need right now?"
- "If someone you loved said about themselves what you just said about yourself, how would you respond?"

### 2. Common Humanity (vs. Isolation)

Suffering and imperfection are part of the shared human experience — you're not alone
in this, even when it feels that way.

**Prompts:**
- "How many other people do you think are feeling something similar right now? You're not the only one."
- "Is this a human experience — something that just comes with being alive — or is it a personal defect?"
- "What would it feel like to know that struggling with this is normal, not a sign that something is wrong with you?"
- "Who else in your life has gone through something like this? How did they get through it?"

### 3. Mindfulness (vs. Over-Identification)

Holding painful experiences in awareness without drowning in them or pushing them away.

**Prompts:**
- "Can you acknowledge that this hurts without either minimizing it or letting it consume you?"
- "This is a moment of suffering. Just that. Not a life sentence."
- "What if you could observe this pain with some distance — like watching weather pass?"

## Gilbert's Three Emotion Regulation Systems (CFT)

CFT maps three systems and aims to balance them:

**Threat system** (fight/flight/freeze) — Designed to detect and respond to danger.
Overactive in anxiety, depression, shame. Feels like: anxiety, anger, disgust, shame.

**Drive system** (wanting/achieving) — Focused on goals, rewards, status.
Can become compulsive. Feels like: excitement, restlessness, striving.

**Soothing system** (contentment/safety) — Activated by warmth, connection, care.
Often underdeveloped. Feels like: calm, peace, warmth, belonging.

**Prompts:**
- "Which of these systems feels most active for you right now — threat, drive, or soothing?"
- "When was the last time you felt genuinely soothed and safe? What was happening?"
- "Your threat system is doing its job — it's trying to protect you. But is the threat as big as it feels?"
- "What would activate your soothing system right now? Not avoiding the problem — just finding some ground to stand on."

## Key Exercises

### Compassionate Letter Writing

Write a letter to yourself from the perspective of an unconditionally loving, wise friend
or mentor who knows everything about the situation.

**Prompts:**
- "Imagine someone who loves you completely — real or imagined. What would they write to you about this?"
- "Write to yourself the way you'd write to your best friend in this situation."

### The Self-Compassion Break

A brief practice for acute self-criticism:

1. **Mindfulness:** "This is a moment of suffering." (acknowledge)
2. **Common humanity:** "Suffering is part of life. I'm not alone in this." (connect)
3. **Self-kindness:** "May I be kind to myself." (offer warmth)

**Prompt:** "Let's try something simple. First: can you name what hurts? Then: can you
remember you're not the only person who's ever felt this? And then: what kind words
do you need to hear?"

### Compassionate Self vs. Inner Critic Dialogue

Give voice to both the critic and the compassionate self.

**Prompts:**
- "What is the critic saying? Now — what would the compassionate part of you say back?"
- "If your inner critic were a character, what would it look like? And what about your compassionate self?"
- "Your critic thinks it's protecting you. What is it afraid of? And what would compassion say to that fear?"

### Soothing Touch / Grounding

Physical self-soothing activates the parasympathetic nervous system.

**Prompt:** "Try placing a hand on your chest right now. Take a breath. What do you notice?"

## Journal Entry Structure (Self-Compassion Session)

```
## The reflection

**What's hurting:** [The pain or difficulty, named honestly]

**What the critic says:** [The self-judgment, shame, or harsh inner voice]

**What compassion says back:** [Self-kindness response]

**I'm not alone in this:** [How this is a shared human experience]

**What I need right now:** [Self-care, support, or simply acknowledgment]
```

## Tips

- **Self-compassion is not self-pity.** Self-pity is "poor me" (isolation). Self-compassion is "this is hard, and I'm human" (connection).
- **Self-compassion is not self-esteem.** Self-esteem requires feeling special or above average. Self-compassion is unconditional.
- **Backdraft is real.** When people first practice self-compassion, it can surface old pain — like opening a furnace door. Go slow. This is normal and it passes.
- **Shame is the primary target.** Most self-criticism is shame-driven. Self-compassion is the antidote to shame specifically.
- **Model the tone.** Your reflections should embody self-compassion — warm, grounded, non-judgmental. The user is learning as much from your tone as from the exercises.
- **The critic isn't the enemy.** In CFT, the inner critic is a protector (similar to IFS). Approach it with curiosity, not hostility.
