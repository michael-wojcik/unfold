# Positive Psychology — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Well-being is not simply the absence of suffering. Flourishing requires actively
cultivating strengths, meaning, positive relationships, engagement, and accomplishment.
Positive psychology provides evidence-based practices for building these.

## Key Frameworks

### PERMA Model (Seligman)

Five pillars of well-being:

- **P — Positive Emotion:** Joy, gratitude, serenity, interest, hope, pride, amusement,
  inspiration, awe, love
- **E — Engagement:** Flow states, deep absorption, using strengths in challenging tasks
- **R — Relationships:** Meaningful connections, feeling loved and valued
- **M — Meaning:** Belonging to and serving something larger than yourself
- **A — Accomplishment:** Mastery, achievement, pursuing goals for their own sake

**Prompt:** "Which of these feels most alive in your life right now? Which feels most
missing?"

### Character Strengths (VIA Classification)

24 strengths in 6 categories. Useful for helping users identify what's already working:

**Wisdom:** Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment, Love of Learning, Perspective
**Courage:** Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest
**Humanity:** Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence
**Justice:** Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership
**Temperance:** Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-Regulation
**Transcendence:** Appreciation of Beauty, Gratitude, Hope, Humor, Spirituality

**Prompts:**
- "When you're at your best, what strengths are you using?"
- "Think of a time you felt really in your element. What were you doing? What made it work?"
- "Which of your strengths have you been neglecting lately?"
- "How could you bring more of [strength] into your everyday life?"

## Exercises for Journaling

### Three Good Things (Gratitude)

A classic evidence-based exercise. At the end of a session or as a check-in:

**Prompts:**
- "What are three things that went well today — even small ones?"
- "For each one: why did it happen? What was your role in it?"
- "What does noticing these things tell you about what you value?"

### Best Possible Self

Visualization exercise useful for values and goal-setting:

**Prompts:**
- "Imagine your life a year from now, where things have gone as well as they realistically could. What does that look like?"
- "Not a fantasy — a realistic best case. What are you doing? Who's around you? How do you feel?"
- "What's one thing about that vision that you could start moving toward now?"

### Savoring

Deliberately paying attention to and amplifying positive experiences:

**Prompts:**
- "Tell me about something good that happened recently. Let's slow down and really sit with it."
- "What did it feel like in the moment? What made it special?"
- "How could you create more moments like that?"

### Strengths Spotting

Identifying strengths in action, especially when the user can't see them:

**Prompts:**
- "You just described handling a really difficult situation. What strengths did that take?"
- "I notice you [specific thing they did]. That takes [strength]. Do you recognize that in yourself?"
- "Where else does that strength show up in your life?"

### Meaningful Activities Audit

When someone feels disconnected from meaning:

**Prompts:**
- "Walk me through a typical day. Which parts feel meaningful? Which feel hollow?"
- "What used to give you a sense of purpose?"
- "If you had an extra two hours a week with no obligations, what would you do with them?"
- "When do you lose track of time? What does that tell you?"

### Acts of Kindness / Connection

Building the R in PERMA:

**Prompts:**
- "Who in your life would benefit from hearing from you today?"
- "What's a small kindness you could do this week that would also light you up?"
- "When's the last time you felt deeply connected to someone? What made that possible?"

## Post-Traumatic Growth

For users who have been through difficulty, positive psychology can help with meaning-making
(but only after the pain has been acknowledged — never skip to silver linings):

**Prompts:**
- "Looking back on that difficult time — has anything unexpected come from it?"
- "Sometimes hardship changes us in ways we didn't choose but can learn from. Has that been true for you?"
- "What do you know now that you didn't know before?"

## Journal Entry Structure (Positive Psych Session)

```
## The reflection

**What I'm grateful for:** [Specific things, with why]

**Strengths I noticed:** [In myself, in action]

**What gives me meaning:** [Activities, relationships, purposes]

**What I want to cultivate:** [Intentional focus going forward]
```

## Tips

- **Positive psychology is NOT toxic positivity.** It's not about ignoring pain — it's about also attending to what's working. Always validate difficulty first.
- **Gratitude works best when specific.** "I'm grateful for my friend Sarah calling to check on me when I was quiet this week" beats "I'm grateful for friends."
- **Strengths are context-dependent.** A strength overused becomes a liability (e.g., perseverance becoming stubbornness). Help users see the nuance.
- **Meaning ≠ happiness.** Meaningful activities can be difficult and even painful. That's okay.
- **Flow requires challenge-skill balance.** If someone feels flat, they might need more challenge, not less stress.
- **Don't prescribe happiness.** Meet the user where they are. Sometimes the most positive thing is honest acknowledgment of struggle.
