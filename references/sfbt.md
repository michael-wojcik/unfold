# Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

You don't always need to understand the problem to find the solution. SFBT focuses on
what's already working, what the person wants instead, and the smallest possible next
step. It assumes competence — the user has solved problems before, has resources, and
knows more about their situation than anyone else.

Particularly useful for quick check-ins and when someone is stuck in problem-saturated
thinking.

## Key Techniques

### The Miracle Question

The signature SFBT intervention — a vivid, detailed exploration of the desired future.

**The classic form:**
"Imagine tonight while you're asleep, a miracle happens, and the problem we've been
talking about is completely solved. But you don't know the miracle happened because
you were asleep. When you wake up tomorrow, what's the first thing you'd notice
that tells you something is different?"

**Follow-ups (go specific):**
- "What else would be different?"
- "Who would notice the change first? What would they see?"
- "What would you be doing differently at work? At home?"
- "How would you feel in your body?"
- "What's the smallest piece of that miracle that's already happening, even a little?"

**Adapted versions for journaling:**
- "If this situation were exactly the way you wanted it, what would tomorrow look like?"
- "Describe your ideal Tuesday in detail — not a vacation fantasy, just a regular day where things are working."
- "What would be the first sign to someone else that things had shifted for you?"

### Exception Finding

Looking for times the problem is absent or less intense. The key SFBT move.

**Prompts:**
- "When was the last time this problem was a little less bad? What was different about that time?"
- "Are there times of day, situations, or people where this doesn't show up as much?"
- "What were you doing differently the last time things felt okay?"
- "What's something you've already tried that helped, even a little?"
- "Tell me about a recent day that went better than average. What made it better?"

### Scaling Questions

Pragmatic, non-judgmental assessment of where someone is and where they want to go.

**Prompts:**
- "On a scale of 0-10, where 10 is the miracle and 0 is the worst it's been, where are you today?"
- "What makes it a [X] and not a [X-1]?" (This surfaces what's already working)
- "What would a [X+1] look like? What's one small thing that would move you up half a point?"
- "What number would be 'good enough' — not perfect, just good enough?"

### Coping Questions

When someone is really struggling — honoring that they're surviving.

**Prompts:**
- "Given everything you're dealing with, how have you managed to keep going?"
- "What's gotten you through the hardest days?"
- "You're here, talking about this. That's not nothing. What does that say about you?"
- "What strengths are you using that you might not be giving yourself credit for?"

### Compliments (Genuine)

SFBT therapists notice and name competence, resilience, and effort.

**Examples:**
- "You've been navigating a really complex situation. That takes a lot of [skill/quality]."
- "The fact that you can see both sides of this tells me something about your clarity."
- "You noticed the pattern yourself — that's not easy to do from the inside."

### Best Hopes

Opening a session with future focus rather than problem focus.

**Prompts:**
- "What are your best hopes for our conversation today?"
- "By the end of this, what would make you feel like it was time well spent?"
- "If you walk away from this reflection feeling just a little bit different, what would that look like?"

## Journal Entry Structure (SFBT Session)

```
## The reflection

**What I want instead:** [The preferred future, described concretely]

**What's already working:** [Exceptions — times the problem is less present]

**Where I am right now:** [Scaling — number + what makes it that number]

**One step forward:** [The smallest move toward the preferred future]

**What's gotten me this far:** [Coping, strengths, resources already in play]
```

## Tips

- **Stay future-focused and solution-focused.** If the user keeps circling back to the problem, gently redirect: "I hear how hard that is. Let's also look at what you want instead."
- **Don't dismiss the problem.** SFBT isn't about ignoring pain. Acknowledge it, then turn toward solutions. "And also — what's working?"
- **Small steps are everything.** SFBT trusts that small changes ripple outward. Don't push for grand action plans.
- **The user is the expert.** Your job is to draw out what they already know, not to prescribe solutions.
- **Great for quick check-ins.** A scaling question + "what would move you up half a point?" is a complete mini-session.
- **Pairs well with other frameworks.** Use SFBT to open a session (best hopes, scaling), then shift to CBT, ACT, etc. for the deeper work, then return to SFBT for next steps.
