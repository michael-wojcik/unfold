# Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Psychological suffering comes not from pain itself but from our struggle against it.
ACT builds psychological flexibility: the ability to be present, open to experience,
and do what matters — even when it's hard.

## The Six Core Processes

### 1. Present Moment Awareness
Being here, now — not lost in the past or future.

**Prompts:**
- "What are you noticing right now, as we talk about this?"
- "Can you stay with that feeling for a moment, without trying to change it?"
- "What's happening in your body as you describe this?"

### 2. Defusion (Unhooking from Thoughts)
Seeing thoughts as thoughts — not as truths or commands.

**Prompts:**
- "What if that thought is just your mind doing its worry thing?"
- "Try this: instead of 'I'm not good enough,' what if you said 'I'm having the thought that I'm not good enough'? Does that feel any different?"
- "Your mind is telling you a story about this. What's the story?"
- "How old is this thought? Has your mind been telling you this for a long time?"

**Key distinction from CBT:** In ACT, we don't debate whether the thought is true or
false. We notice it, acknowledge it, and ask whether it's *helpful* to hold tightly.

### 3. Acceptance (Willingness)
Making room for difficult experiences rather than fighting them.

**Prompts:**
- "What would it be like to just... let that feeling be there, without needing to fix it?"
- "You've been fighting this feeling for a while. What has that cost you?"
- "What if the feeling isn't the problem — what if the struggle against it is?"
- "Can you make a little room for this, even if it's uncomfortable?"

### 4. Self-as-Context (The Observing Self)
You are the sky, not the weather. Experiences pass through you.

**Prompts:**
- "You've felt this before and it passed. What does the part of you that watches all this come and go notice?"
- "If you step back and just observe — not judge, not fix, just watch — what do you see?"
- "These feelings are real, and they're also temporary. What remains constant underneath?"

### 5. Values Clarification
What matters to you, deep down? What kind of person do you want to be?

**Prompts:**
- "Underneath all the stress, what do you actually care about here?"
- "If this problem were magically solved, what would you do with your energy?"
- "When you're at your best, what are you like? What drives that?"
- "Imagine looking back on this period from years in the future. What would you want to have done?"
- "What would you be doing differently if fear weren't a factor?"

**Values vs. Goals:** Values are directions (being a loving partner), goals are
destinations (getting married). Values can never be completed.

### 6. Committed Action
Taking steps aligned with values, even in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings.

**Prompts:**
- "Given what matters to you, what's one small step you could take?"
- "You can feel scared and still act. What would a values-consistent move look like here?"
- "What's the smallest possible action that moves you toward what matters?"

## The Choice Point

A useful ACT framework for journaling:

```
         [Difficult thoughts/feelings appear]
                    |
              Choice Point
               /         \
    Away moves            Toward moves
    (avoidance,           (values-aligned,
     numbing,              even when hard)
     withdrawal)
         |                      |
    Short-term relief      Long-term vitality
    Long-term stuckness    Short-term discomfort
```

**Prompt:** "When this feeling shows up, what do you usually do? Does that move you
toward or away from what matters to you?"

## Journal Entry Structure (ACT Session)

```
## The reflection

**What showed up:** [The difficult thoughts, feelings, or experiences]

**The struggle:** [How I've been fighting or avoiding this]

**What I noticed when I stopped fighting:** [Willingness, defusion]

**What matters underneath:** [Values identified]

**A toward move:** [Committed action, even small]
```

## Tips

- ACT is experiential — use metaphors and exercises, not just talk
- "Willingness" is not wanting or liking — it's making room
- Don't force acceptance. Acceptance is an invitation, not a demand
- Values work can be emotional — it often reveals grief about time lost or paths not taken
- The goal is not to feel better — it's to feel *fully* and live well
