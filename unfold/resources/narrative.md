# Narrative Therapy — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

We make sense of our lives through stories. When the dominant story about ourselves
becomes problem-saturated ("I'm a failure," "I always mess up relationships"), it
crowds out other truths. Narrative therapy externalizes problems, identifies unique
outcomes, and helps people re-author their story — reclaiming agency over their own
narrative.

Journaling is an inherently narrative act, making this framework a natural fit.

## Key Techniques

### Externalization

The central move: separate the person from the problem. The problem is the problem —
the person is not the problem.

**Prompts:**
- "If this problem had a name, what would you call it?"
- "When does [the problem] show up most? When does it have the most power over you?"
- "How long has [the problem] been influencing your life? How did it first get a foothold?"
- "What does [the problem] tell you about yourself? And do you think that's the full story?"
- "What tactics does [the problem] use to keep you stuck?"

**Language shift:** Instead of "I'm anxious," → "Anxiety showed up today." Instead of
"I'm lazy," → "The procrastination story has been loud this week." This subtle shift
creates distance and agency.

### Unique Outcomes (Exceptions)

Moments that contradict the dominant story — times the person resisted, coped, or
acted differently than the problem would predict.

**Prompts:**
- "Has there been a time recently when [the problem] tried to show up but you didn't let it run the show?"
- "When was the last time you surprised yourself — did something the old story would say you couldn't?"
- "Are there people in your life who see a different version of you than the one [the problem] describes?"
- "What would that moment say about who you're becoming?"

### Re-authoring

Building an alternative story from unique outcomes. Thickening the counter-narrative.

**Prompts:**
- "If the version of you who handled that situation were the main character, what kind of story would this be?"
- "What title would you give the chapter you're writing right now?"
- "Looking at these moments where you showed up differently — what do they have in common? What do they say about your values?"
- "If you were writing the next chapter, how would you want it to go? Not a fantasy — what feels true to where you're heading?"

### Witnessing and Audience

Who sees and validates the preferred story?

**Prompts:**
- "Who in your life would be least surprised to hear about this strength in you?"
- "If someone who loves you were watching that moment, what would they say?"
- "Whose opinion matters to you in this? What would they notice about how you've changed?"

### Documenting the Preferred Story

Narrative therapy has a tradition of therapeutic letters and documents — journaling
is a perfect vehicle.

**Prompts:**
- "If you were writing a letter to yourself from six months in the future, what would future-you want to say?"
- "What's one sentence you'd like to be true about yourself? Not a lie — something that's starting to be true."

## Journal Entry Structure (Narrative Session)

```
## The reflection

**The story that's been running:** [The dominant narrative — externalized]

**How it got its power:** [When/how this story took hold]

**Moments that don't fit the old story:** [Unique outcomes, exceptions]

**The story I'm starting to tell instead:** [Re-authored narrative]

**Who sees this version of me:** [Witnesses to the preferred story]
```

## Tips

- **Externalization takes practice.** Model the language shift in your reflections: "It sounds like the self-doubt story was really loud today."
- **Don't impose a positive story.** The re-authored narrative must come from the user's lived experience, not your optimism. Built from actual unique outcomes, not wishful thinking.
- **Thin vs. thick description.** Problem stories are often "thin" (flat, one-dimensional). The work is to "thicken" the alternative story with detail, context, and meaning.
- **Journaling IS re-authoring.** The act of writing itself is a narrative act. How we choose to tell our story shapes how we experience it.
- **Cultural sensitivity.** Narrative therapy was developed with deep attention to power and culture. Some "problem stories" are imposed by systems, not just internal patterns.
