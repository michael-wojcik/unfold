# Existential Therapy — Journaling Reference

## Core Principle

Anxiety and struggle are not disorders — they're responses to the fundamental
conditions of being alive. Existential therapy engages directly with life's biggest
questions: meaning, freedom, isolation, and death. Rather than pathologizing these
concerns, it treats them as the raw material of an examined life.

This framework works best when someone is grappling with big-picture questions — not
everyday stress, but the deeper "what's it all for?" moments.

## Yalom's Four Ultimate Concerns

### 1. Death (Mortality and Finitude)

Awareness that life ends shapes how we live. Confronting mortality can be paralyzing
or liberating.

**Prompts:**
- "If you knew you had one year left, what would change about how you're living right now?"
- "What are you putting off because you're acting like you have forever?"
- "Does the awareness that time is limited clarify anything for you about what matters?"
- "What do you want to have mattered when you look back on this period?"

**Not for acute grief or suicidal ideation.** These prompts are for existential
reflection when someone is grappling with meaning, not for someone in crisis.

### 2. Freedom (and Responsibility)

We are freer than we admit — and that freedom is terrifying because it comes with
total responsibility. No one else can live your life for you.

**Prompts:**
- "In this situation, what are your actual options — not just the ones that feel safe?"
- "If you take away 'I have to' and 'I should' — what do you actually choose?"
- "What would it mean to fully own this decision, including the consequences?"
- "Where in your life are you acting as if you have no choice, when actually you do?"
- "What are you choosing by not choosing?"

### 3. Isolation (Existential Aloneness)

No matter how connected we are, there's an unbridgeable gap between ourselves and
others. We each experience the world alone.

**Prompts:**
- "Is there something you're going through that feels impossible to fully share with anyone else?"
- "What's the difference between being lonely and being alone? Where are you right now?"
- "When do you feel most seen by another person? What makes that possible?"
- "Is there a part of your experience that you've never been able to put into words for someone else?"

### 4. Meaninglessness (The Search for Meaning)

The universe doesn't hand us meaning. We have to create it — and that's both daunting
and empowering.

**Prompts:**
- "What gives your life meaning right now? And is that enough?"
- "If you woke up tomorrow and everything felt meaningless, what would you try to reconnect with first?"
- "Are you living for something, or just getting through?"
- "What would you create, build, or contribute if you knew it would outlast you?"
- "What's the difference between happiness and meaning for you? Which are you chasing?"

## Additional Existential Themes

### Authenticity

Living in alignment with your true self rather than performing for others.

**Prompts:**
- "Where in your life are you performing a version of yourself that doesn't feel real?"
- "What would the most authentic version of you do in this situation?"
- "What would you stop doing if you stopped caring what people thought?"
- "When do you feel most like yourself?"

### Groundlessness and Anxiety

Existential anxiety isn't a bug — it's a signal that you're confronting something real.

**Prompts:**
- "This anxiety might not be a problem to solve. What if it's telling you something important?"
- "What uncertainty are you sitting with right now? What would it be like to stay with it instead of rushing to resolve it?"
- "Sometimes the ground feels like it's shifting under your feet. What helps you stand anyway?"

### Choice and Commitment

Choosing one path means closing others. This is a form of loss, and it's normal to grieve it.

**Prompts:**
- "What are you saying no to by saying yes to this?"
- "Is the fear of choosing wrong keeping you from choosing at all?"
- "What would it look like to commit fully — not because you're certain, but because you've decided?"

## Journal Entry Structure (Existential Session)

```
## The reflection

**The big question:** [What existential theme surfaced — meaning, freedom, mortality, isolation]

**What I'm sitting with:** [The discomfort, uncertainty, or clarity that emerged]

**What this reveals about what matters:** [Values, priorities, what I want to be about]

**How I want to live in response:** [Not a fix — a direction, an orientation]
```

## Tips

- **Don't rush to comfort.** Existential work requires tolerating discomfort. Sitting with meaninglessness is part of the process — don't paper over it with platitudes.
- **This isn't nihilism.** Existential therapy takes meaning seriously — it just says you have to build it yourself rather than find it pre-made.
- **Meet philosophical questions philosophically.** If someone asks "What's the point?", take the question seriously rather than treating it as a symptom.
- **Pairs well with ACT.** ACT's values clarification is essentially applied existentialism. Move between the philosophical and the practical.
- **Pairs well with Narrative.** "What story are you living?" is both a narrative and existential question.
- **Not for everyone, not for every session.** Some people aren't drawn to this level of abstraction, and that's fine. Don't push existential depth on someone who wants practical help.
- **Watch for spiritual/religious sensitivity.** These questions overlap with spiritual territory. Be respectful of the user's worldview and don't impose a secular or religious frame.
